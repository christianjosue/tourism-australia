Experience of
traveling

Although we encounter many cultural differences in our travels, it is actually incredible how similar we are to people who live across the globe. Despite our diverse cultural backgrounds and daily lifestyles, travel allows us to connect with others and realize that we actually have a lot in common with that one friend living in Sweden. We also enjoy spending time with family, listening to indie pop music, and dancing on the weekends.
Once we’ve pulled ourselves outside of the safe, protective bubble of home, we can clearly see that we are just like those people hundreds of miles away. We are all a part of a bigger story; we all have more bringing us together than tearing us apart. It’s this realization that breaks down fear and prejudices, making the world a much smaller, more welcoming, and connected place.

Traveling helps us feel like our best self because we are more willing to receive the world’s many lessons, no matter their shape or size. It helps us recognize our shared humanity with others and dissipates fear or misunderstandings. After all, it’s much more fun to love the world than to be afraid of it.
In travel, we find the best in ourselves and in others. We become the person that we had hoped we would become as a child: an explorer, open to meeting new people, and learning new things. And maybe, just maybe, someone who is brave enough to eat scorpions off a stick.

Paradise beaches

Day 1

Byron Bay

The city has several specialized beaches for surfing. It is a popular tourist sport among national and international tourists. The Cape Byron Lighthouse is one of the city's attractions, helping with whale watching, which in turn is a very important industry and contributor to the local economy.

Temperate and tropical waters abound in Byron Bay, making it a popular area for diving and snorkelling.

Day 2

Whitehaven

The 7 kilometer long White Haven beach in Australia is located on Whitsunday Island and is considered the most ecologically friendly beach in the world, the cleanest beach on the planet and even considered one of the most paradisiacal beaches on Earth.

This Australian beach got the 1st position in this study that named the eight most eco-friendly beaches on planet earth.

Day 3

Cable beach

Cable Beach, in Broome, in the Kimberley region of Western Australia, is a 22 kilometer long beach on a stretch of white sand, set against a backdrop of ocher and red cliffs and fringed by the turquoise waters of the Indian Ocean.

A camel ride along the beach at sunrise or sunset is a must, immersing yourself in the pearly romantic history of the Kimberley town and its multicultural society.

Day 4

Wineglass Bay

It is located on the east coast of Tasmania, surrounded by the Freycinet National Park, a protected area with exceptional flora and fauna.
Wineglass is famous for its perfect horseshoe shape, white sand and turquoise waters. But also because of its natural environment, surrounded by bays and mountains, far from the bustle of other typical beaches. It is worth doing the Freycinet Experience Walk, a path that surrounds the entire area.

Day 5

Palm Cove

Palm Cove is a tiny beachside village centred around an esplanade lined with 500-year-old melaleuca trees. Walk around barefoot, choose a bike instead of a car and buy fresh seafood for a sunset barbecue on the beach. You can hire a kayak and paddle right off the beach or take a tour to nearby Double Island. The Palm Cove jetty is a great fishing spot, with plenty of Spanish mackerel, cod, whiting, stripeys and flathead to be caught.

Day 6

Twelve Apostles

The Twelve Apostles is the name given to a grouping of limestone needles that protrude from the sea on the coast of Port Campbell National Park, in Victoria, Australia.

They were formed by rock erosion and movement along the Great Ocean Road. The proximity of one to the other is the cause of the curiosity of the place and what has made it an important and popular tourist attraction.

Wildlife adventure

Day 1

Kangaroo

Kangaroo Island (from English: Kangaroo Island) is the third largest island in Australia, after the island of Tasmania and Melville Island. It is 112 km southwest of Adelaide (Australia), at the entrance to the Gulf of Saint Vincent. Its closest point to the mainland is 13 km from Cape Jervis, at the tip of the Fleurieu peninsula, in the state of South Australia. The island is 150 km long and between 900 m and 57 km wide; comprises an area of 4,405 km² and has 540 km of coastline, its highest point being Prospect Hill, with 307 m.

Day 2

Koala

Philip Island, one of the best islands in Australia where you can find koalas. It is quite easy to visit because it is close to Melbourne so you can go and return in the same day.

They have a network of walkways that allow you to walk through the eucalyptus trees at the height where the Koalas live, therefore it is a great place to observe them in their environment.

Day 3

Humpback whale

The picturesque seaside town of Augusta is one of the best locations in Australia to view Humpback Whales as they make their epic migration north, filling the beautiful Flinders Bay area.

Each year from late May, you will experience male Humpback Whales competing for the attention of females through various behaviours including breaching and thunderous tail slaps.

Day 4

Tasmania

The Tasmanian devil is a species of dasiuromorph marsupial in the Dasyuridae family.

Another of Australia's mythical, you can only find him in the nature reserves of Tasmania. It is the largest carnivorous marsupial, such as a cat or a small dog, in existence today, it gives off a super strong odor and screams like crazy. So... a small animal that has well earned its name of "demon".

Day 5

Dingo

If you are in Sydney and want to see the Australian dingo up close, a good place to do so is in the Blue Mountains, about 50km west of the city. On the other hand, throughout the country there are some parks and sanctuaries specialized in this type of fauna, such as the Healesville Sanctuary, located in Melbourne.

Anatomically, to the naked eye, an Australian Dingo can be very similar to a domestic dog. However, the dingo really is a one-of-a-kind canid.

Day 6

Wombat

This is the wombat, a small Australian herbivorous marsupial (about 25 kilos in weight) that is very friendly to humans because of its chubby appearance with short legs (it could perfectly be a stuffed animal) and that lives in burrows.

You will be able to see them in their natural habitat in the Blue Mountains, among other places, although it is not easy.

From sidney to melbourne

Day 1

Sidney

Sydney, the capital of New South Wales and one of Australia's largest cities, is famous for its harborside Sydney Opera House, with its distinctive sail-shaped design. Massive Darling Harbor and smaller Circular Quay are hubs of riverside life, with the arching Sydney Harbor Bridge and the renowned Royal Botanic Gardens nearby. Sydney Tower's outer platform, the Skywalk, offers 360-degree views of the city and suburbs.

Day 2

Canberra

The location of Canberra was selected for the position of the national capital in 1908 as a compromise between Sydney and Melbourne, the two great cities. It is different from other Australian cities in that it was built from scratch, as a planned city.

It owns the vast majority of government and political departments, such as Australia's parliament and supreme court.

Day 3

Lake hume

Lake Hume is an impressive body of water held by an awe-inspiring dam wall and is an easy 15-minute drive from Albury Wodonga. There’s something for everybody at Lake Hume. For the adventurous, there is wakeboarding, water-skiing, tube-riding and stand-up paddleboarding. For those who like a gentler pace, the foreshore is the perfect place to enjoy a picnic, fire up a barbecue and experience the mountain views while you connect with friends and family.

Day 4

Kosciuszko Park

Yes, it’s home to Australia’s highest peak, but that’s not all: Kosciuszko National Park offers travellers a whole world of spectacular to explore. Three ski resorts (and a fourth currently being rebuilt) are the centre of human activity here: ski or snowboard the country’s highest runs, or test your legs on a cross-country skiing odyssey. Take on big-challenge walks, saddle up and ride the High Plains, explore caves, or just find a people-free lookout and simply breathe in the beauty and tranquillity.

Day 5

Kiama

It could be the rhythmic hum of the breaking waves, the reassuring wink from the iconic lighthouse, or the ‘whoosh’ from one of the world’s biggest blowholes. Kiama’s relaxing coastal vibe has an instant effect. The name Kiama is from its Wodi Wodi Indigenous name Kiaram-a, to which some sources give the meaning “where the sea makes a noise”.
Kiama boasts several popular surfing beaches and caravan parks, and numerous outdoor cafes and restaurants.

Day 6

Melbourne

Melbourne is the most European city in Australia and certainly the most cosmopolitan. It has been chosen for many consecutive years as the best city in the world to live.
It is also a very large and multicultural city; With a population of close to 5 million, Melbourne has plenty of job opportunities. In its surroundings there are an infinity of national parks that will make you fall in love and will allow you to meet all kinds of wildlife. It's an amazing city!
